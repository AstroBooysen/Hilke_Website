Welcome
I am Hilke
Brockmann

I study the questions that keep us up at night: Will I be happy? Will I be treated fairly? What happens when we grow old? And what does the digital revolution mean for all of us? As a sociologist, I approach these profoundly human concerns with rigorous empirical methods — and a genuine conviction that science should matter beyond the academy.

For over two decades I have been a professor at Constructor University Bremen, with research stays at Harvard, the European University Institute in Florence, and institutions across Africa and Asia. My work has tracked happiness trajectories across entire lifetimes, examined why immigrants often report higher wellbeing than the people around them, and revealed the hidden inequalities shaping women's careers. Most recently, I am pushing into new territory: using AI-assisted text analytics, digital trace data, and computational methods to understand how we age, how societies polarize, and how technology reshapes the way we live and feel.

Communicating research is not a side project — it is central to what I do. I collaborate across disciplines and continents, supervise a lively group of PhD students, and bring sociological thinking into public debate, policy conversations, and the media whenever I can.

Selected Publications

Books & Papers

A curated selection of research across the themes of happiness, inequality, aging, and digital society — chosen for intellectual impact, originality, and accessibility.

2026

Journal Article

Females Welcome! When immigrants make the local population happy

Journal of Economic Psychology, 113 (with R. Fernandez-Urbano)

A striking finding: the arrival of immigrant women boosts subjective wellbeing in host communities. A genuinely surprising result that reframes public debates around migration and social cohesion.

2025

Journal Article

The Pursuit of Happiness at the End of Life: Research on Connection, Care, and Meaning in Hospice Settings

The Mind, 2

One of the first rigorous empirical studies of what actually makes people happy in their final months — an urgent question for aging societies everywhere. This paper opened a new research program.

2024

Edited Volume

Encyclopedia of Happiness, Quality of Life, and Subjective Wellbeing

Edward Elgar, Cheltenham (edited with R. Fernandez-Urbano)

The field's most comprehensive reference work — a landmark synthesis co-edited by Brockmann that brings together the world's leading scholars on wellbeing research. Essential for researchers, policymakers, and curious readers alike.

2018

Journal Article

Why managerial women are less happy than managerial men

Journal of Happiness Studies, 19/3: 755–779 (with A.M. Koch, A. Diederich, C. Edling)

A widely cited study that puts data behind a phenomenon many women recognize — and opens hard questions about the real costs of professional success. Accessible, provocative, and policy-relevant.

2016

Journal Article

Happy taxation: Increasing tax compliance through positive rewards?

Journal of Public Policy, 36/3: 381–406 (with L. Seelkopf, P. Genschel)

A beautifully unexpected research question — can reward-based nudges replace penalties in tax compliance? This paper bridges happiness research and public policy in a way that genuinely surprised policymakers.

2013

Edited Volume

Is More Always Better? Human Happiness and the Pursuit of Maximization

Springer, Heidelberg & New York (edited with J. Delhey)

A thought-provoking interdisciplinary inquiry into the limits of "more is better" — from wealth to ambition to information. One of the books that established Brockmann as a leading voice in happiness research.

2009

Journal Article

The China puzzle: Falling happiness in a rising economy

Journal of Happiness Studies, 10: 387–405 (with J. Delhey, C. Welzel)

A landmark paper: economic growth does not automatically produce wellbeing. This counterintuitive finding for China resonated globally and remains one of the field's most-cited studies on the happiness–GDP paradox.

2004

Journal Article

Love and death in Germany: The marital biography and its impact on mortality

Journal of Marriage & the Family, 66/3: 567–581 (with T. Klein)

An early, award-winning study using large longitudinal data to show that intimate relationships profoundly shape how long we live — still widely read, still cited, still important.

Current Research

Projects

Four research programs — each tackling a question that matters to individuals and societies, and each pushing the boundaries of sociological methods.

Aging & Wellbeing 01

Happiness at the End of Life

What does happiness mean when life is drawing to a close — and how can societies help people experience dignity, connection, and meaning rather than fear? This project combines life-course survey data and administrative health records with digital trace data and NLP-based text analysis to map how emotional wellbeing, life satisfaction, and social relationships evolve in people's final years. In close collaboration with palliative-care clinicians, we translate findings directly into recommendations for end-of-life care and social support policy.

AI & Society 02

Gender-Inclusive AI

AI is not neutral — it reflects the values, priorities, and blind spots of the people who build it. Since women remain dramatically underrepresented in AI development, this project asks: What happens when female perspectives and care-oriented principles are designed into AI systems from the start? Using algorithmic auditing, survey experiments, and behavioral data, we test whether gender-inclusive design produces AI that works better for everyone. Embedded in an international collaboration spanning Estonia, France, and Sweden.

Immigration & Integration 03

Technology, Integration, and Immigrant Wellbeing

Can digital tools make integration faster, fairer, and more humane? This comparative project examines how AI-driven job matching, digital credential recognition, and e-government services affect immigrants' employment outcomes, occupational mobility, and subjective wellbeing — in Europe and, via an Australian collaboration, across radically different policy regimes. Using linked employer–employee datasets and machine-learning methods, we identify the technological conditions that genuinely support a better life after migration.

Gender & Career — Ongoing 04

Discrimination of Mothers: Career, Care, and the Motherhood Penalty

Becoming a mother remains one of the most consequential career events a woman can experience — and one of the most consequential for her happiness. This long-running research program uses longitudinal data to trace how the transition to motherhood affects employment trajectories, earnings, wellbeing, and political representation. We document the structural mechanisms behind the "motherhood penalty" — and look for the policies and workplace cultures that actually make a difference.

Are you a mother who has experienced career discrimination? I am always interested in hearing from women whose experiences can inform this research. Please feel free to reach out — your story matters.

Beyond the University

Outreach & Impact

Good research deserves a wide audience. I actively bring sociological findings into public debate, policy advice, and the media — because the questions I study belong to everyone.

Media & Public Debate

From the Frankfurter Rundschau to Die ZEIT, from the Economist to ZDF and ARTE, my research on happiness, inequality, and aging has reached hundreds of thousands of readers and viewers. I contribute a regular column to the Frankfurter Rundschau (Gastwirtschaft, since 2019), translate complex findings into language that resonates, and welcome journalists who want serious sociological perspective on the news.

Keynotes & Public Lectures

I have given invited talks at Harvard's Center for European Studies, the European University Institute, McKinsey, the Sparkassenverband, and at the Studienstiftung des deutschen Volkes, among many others — as well as at international sociology conferences across North America, Europe, and Asia. My talks combine rigorous evidence with genuinely surprising findings, and are designed to spark conversation rather than close it down.

Policy Consulting & Advisory

I have advised the Coca-Cola Happiness Institute in Berlin on the measurement and communication of wellbeing research, and contributed expert consultation to hospitals and healthcare organizations on quality assurance and age-related healthcare policy. I am a member of the editorial board of the Journal of Happiness Studies and a reviewer for leading journals in sociology, public health, and social policy.
