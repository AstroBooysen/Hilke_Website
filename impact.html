Impact & Societal Relevance

Hilke Brockmann’s research is designed not only to generate publishable findings, but also to produce actionable insights for democratic societies facing polarization, demographic change, digital disruption, and growing inequality. Across policy, health, social innovation, industry, and civil society, the work asks a common question: what do people need to live well together?

Policy

Policy Implications

The major research lines carry direct implications for public policy in Germany and beyond. Happiness and aging research informs evidence-based strategies for age-friendly welfare systems, digital inclusion programs for older adults, and end-of-life care design.

Migration and well-being research provides an empirical foundation for skills-based immigration reform, integration policy, and social cohesion strategies, particularly in receiving societies confronting population aging and labor shortages.

Research on meritocratic beliefs helps explain how citizens perceive inequality, redistribution, and social mobility. This has direct relevance for democratic legitimacy, public communication, and the design of fair institutions.

The AI for Good projects generate evidence and deployable tools relevant to electoral integrity, civic education, occupational safety regulation, and municipal governance.

Innovation

Social Innovation

The research portfolio is not confined to academic publication. It is also oriented toward practical tools, prototypes, and institutional platforms that can be used beyond the university.

  • The DemY2Go wearable AI chatbot is conceived as a low-threshold civic tool deployable by NGOs, municipalities, schools, and party foundations to support deliberative democracy at scale.
  • The translation technology field experiments can generate practical implementation guides for works councils, HR departments, transport providers, and municipal services seeking to improve inclusion and social cohesion in multilingual settings.
  • A future Happiness Lab at Constructor University could serve as an institutional platform translating scientific insights on well-being into evidence-based recommendations for individuals, communities, and policymakers.

The broader aim is to turn social science and AI research into tools that help people live better together in complex democratic societies.

Health

Health Care Innovation

Subjective well-being research has substantial implications for health care systems. The work connects happiness science to population health, mental health policy, preventive care, and healthy aging interventions.

Research on loneliness, social isolation, digital participation, and quality of life helps build the evidence base for more preventive and socially responsive health policy. This is especially relevant in aging societies and in contexts where mental health and social connectedness are major public health concerns.

In this way, the research contributes to a broader understanding of health that includes not only illness and treatment, but also well-being, belonging, and the social conditions of a good life.

Partnerships

Industry and Civil Society Connections

Prior and ongoing connections to relevant sectors support the practical relevance of the research portfolio.

  • AOK (Allgemeine Ortskrankenkasse) is a natural partner for translating happiness and well-being research into health promotion programs and well-being metrics relevant to public health insurance.
  • Coca-Cola Germany represents prior industry collaboration on consumer well-being and happiness research.
  • Political parties and democratic foundations are relevant partners for the AI-mediated deliberation and polarization projects.

These connections strengthen the pathway from academic insight to institutional and social application.

Democracy

Relevance for Democratic Societies

The entire research portfolio is united by a commitment to understanding and strengthening the conditions for good lives in democratic societies. From the psychological foundations of well-being to the social infrastructure of deliberation, from the dynamics of migration to the governance of AI, each project addresses a dimension of the question: what do people need to live well together?

This relevance is not peripheral but constitutive. The research is designed to produce not only rigorous scholarship, but also insights that can help democratic societies respond to polarization, demographic change, digital disruption, and inequality in thoughtful and evidence-based ways.
