Projects

Project 1: Democracy by Design – A Wearable AI for Deliberation, Compromise, and Digital Resilience

Core Idea

Democracies are under strain from polarization, misinformation, and declining trust. This project translates recent breakthroughs in political science and AI research into a wearable, multimodal AI system that supports citizens in real-time political dialogue, compromise-building, and digital selfdefense.

Scientific Foundation

Large-scale experimental studies show that AI-mediated moderation can outperform human facilitation in deliberative settings by reducing polarization, integrating minority perspectives, and increasing perceived fairness and legitimacy (Argyle et al. 2023; Tessler et al. 2024). At the same time, research in ethics and science and technology studies emphasizes that democratic technologies must be value-sensitive by design, embedding transparency, inclusivity, and privacy from the outset (Friedman 1996; Helbing et al. 2023).

Innovation and Design

The project develops an open-source, wearable AI chatbot for smartphones. It integrates real-time translation, argument-structuring assistance, consensus detection, and cybersecurity warnings. Development follows a participatory bootcamp model and is evaluated in real-world scenarios addressing polarization, intergenerational justice, and digital security literacy. Legal scholars ensure compliance with GDPR and the EU AI Act throughout the design and testing process.

Why Funders Should Care

With an investment of approximately €120,000, this project pioneers a new class of civic infrastructure: low-threshold, scalable, and ethically governed. It produces both high-impact academic outputs and a deployable prototype for NGOs, municipalities, and educational institutions—offering a concrete, evidence-based response to democratic erosion at a fraction of the cost of large platform interventions.

Project 2: Language, Belonging, and Happiness in Multicultural Public Life

Core Idea

Everyday public spaces—buses and trains, markets, cafés, civic service points—are among the few remaining arenas of spontaneous social encounter. Simultaneously, Germany faces two parallel trends: growing linguistic diversity and rising social isolation, with a large and increasing share of the population living alone and reporting loneliness. This project investigates whether voluntary, privacy-preserving real-time translation technologies can function as a form of social micro-infrastructure that reduces feelings of exclusion and increases momentary happiness, social comfort, and perceived belonging in multilingual public encounters—without pressuring anyone to abandon their mother tongue. The focus is not on assimilation, but on whether mutual intelligibility can improve everyday well-being for both locals and migrants.

Scientific Foundation

Loneliness and social isolation are now recognized as major public-health and societal challenges in Germany and other advanced societies (Hawkley & Cacioppo 2010; BMFSFJ 2023). Living alone and reduced everyday social contact are strongly associated with lower subjective well-being and life satisfaction. Classic and contemporary intergroup contact research shows that even brief, lowthreshold encounters shape trust, comfort, and social cohesion (Allport 1954; Pettigrew & Tropp 2006). Language barriers are known to restrict access and participation in institutional settings, yet their affective consequences in ordinary public spaces remain largely unexplored. At the same time, experimental research demonstrates that AI-mediated communication and facilitation can improve social outcomes in deliberative and interactional contexts (Argyle et al. 2023; Tessler et al. 2024). What is missing is causal field evidence on whether translation technologies can improve subjective well-being and inclusion in real-world, low-stakes encounters.

Innovation and Design

We implement a randomized field experiment in multilingual public and semi-public spaces. Place × time blocks (e.g., specific train runs, market zones, café sections, or service hours) are randomly assigned to a translation-available condition or a control condition. In the treatment, a visible, opt-in translation affordance (QR code with simple instructions) enables two-way speech translation on participants’ own devices, accompanied by a neutral norm cue emphasizing voluntariness and privacy. No audio content is recorded; the study follows strict GDPR and ethics-by-design principles.

Participants are recruited via brief intercept surveys immediately following shared presence or short interactions. The primary outcome is momentary happiness and affect, measured using short, validated scales commonly used in well-being research (e.g., Kahneman et al., 2004). Secondary outcomes include perceived inclusion or exclusion, social ease, willingness to engage in future contact, and brief interactional behaviors. Mixed-effects models account for clustering by site and time and allow heterogeneity analyses (e.g., people living alone vs. not, prior intergroup contact). The modular design allows scaling to additional cities or settings.

Why Funders Should Care

With a budget of €50,000–€100,000, this project addresses two pressing societal challenges— loneliness and social cohesion in diverse societies—through a low-cost, scalable, and evidencedriven intervention. It generates the first causal evidence on whether translation technologies can improve everyday happiness and belonging in public life, moving beyond abstract debates about integration. For funders, this represents a highly efficient investment: modest resources, strong scientific rigor, and clear implications for municipalities, transport providers, and civil society actors seeking practical tools to strengthen social cohesion.

Project 3: Breaking Language Barriers at Work – Field Experiments on Real-Time Translation Technologies

Core Idea

Globalized labor markets are increasingly multilingual, yet language barriers remain a major source of inequality, safety risks, and exclusion in everyday work processes. Misunderstandings disproportionately affect migrant workers and are especially consequential in industrial, logistical, and shift-based environments. This project provides the first causal field evidence on whether earbud-based, real-time speech-to-speech translation technologies actually improve workplace communication, trust, inclusion, and well-being—or whether their technological promise exceeds their real-world value.

Scientific Foundation

Research in organizational sociology and occupational health shows that linguistic exclusion undermines safety, coordination, and social belonging at work, with particularly strong effects in manual, hazardous, and time-critical occupations (Hardy & Schraepen 2024). At the same time, recent advances in speech-to-speech AI have substantially reduced latency and improved accuracy under controlled conditions. Yet, their performance in noisy, high-stakes work environments remains empirically untested. Diversity and inclusion research further demonstrates that perceived inclusion climates strongly shape trust, empathy, performance, and retention in teams (Holmes et al. 2021; Ward-Bartlett et al. 2023; Ayele et al. 2024).

Innovation and Design

We conduct randomized field experiments in real-world workplaces and educational settings, comparing teams that use real-time translation with control teams that do not. The design combines pre/post measurement, experience sampling during workdays, and mixed-effects modeling. Importantly, no audio content is recorded; only minimal technical meta-events (e.g., latency, dropouts) are logged, ensuring strict data minimization and full GDPR compliance. This allows us to estimate not only average treatment effects, but also boundary conditions—such as noise levels, task types, and latency thresholds—under which translation technologies help, do nothing, or even hinder coordination.

Why Funders Should Care

For an investment of approximately €40,000, this project delivers unusually high value: rigorous causal evidence, immediate relevance for occupational safety and fair integration, and concrete guidance for responsible digitalization. Rather than blindly promoting technology, the project identifies where translation tools are worth deploying and where they are not, preventing costly disinvestment by firms and public employers. Deliverables include open scientific outputs and a practical implementation guide for works councils and HR departments, turning cutting-edge AI into measurable improvements in “good work.”

Across all three projects, the unifying contribution is evidence-based, ethically grounded innovation. Rather than asking whether AI is powerful, we identify the conditions under which it genuinely improves work, social belonging, and democratic participation—and where it should not be used. This focus on causal evidence, modest budgets, and real-world applicability makes the portfolio especially attractive for private funders seeking high-impact, socially responsible investments with clear pathways to implementation.
