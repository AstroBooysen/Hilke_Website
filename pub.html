Current Projects

Evidence-based, ethically grounded work on democracy, belonging, translation, well-being, and social inclusion.

Project 1

Democracy by Design – A Wearable AI for Deliberation, Compromise, and Digital Resilience

Core idea

Democracies are under strain from polarization, misinformation, and declining trust. I am developing a wearable, multimodal AI system that supports citizens in real-time political dialogue, compromise-building, and digital self-defense.

Scientific foundation

Recent large-scale experiments suggest that AI-mediated moderation can reduce polarization, integrate minority perspectives, and increase perceived fairness and legitimacy in deliberative settings.

Innovation and design

The project develops an open-source AI chatbot for smartphones with real-time translation, argument structuring, consensus detection, and cybersecurity warnings, evaluated in real-world scenarios.

Why it matters

The aim is to create low-threshold, scalable, and ethically governed civic infrastructure for NGOs, municipalities, and educational institutions.

Estimated budget: approximately €120,000
Project 2

Language, Belonging, and Happiness in Multicultural Public Life

Core idea

This project investigates whether privacy-preserving real-time translation can reduce exclusion and increase momentary happiness, comfort, and belonging in multilingual public encounters.

Scientific foundation

Loneliness, reduced everyday social contact, and language barriers affect well-being and social cohesion. What is missing is causal evidence from ordinary public spaces.

Innovation and design

I use randomized field experiments in multilingual public and semi-public spaces to test whether translation availability changes happiness, inclusion, and willingness for future contact.

Why it matters

The project addresses loneliness and social cohesion through a low-cost, scalable intervention with practical relevance for municipalities, transport providers, and civil society.

Estimated budget: €50,000–€100,000
Project 3

Breaking Language Barriers at Work – Field Experiments on Real-Time Translation Technologies

Core idea

Global labor markets are increasingly multilingual, yet language barriers remain a major source of inequality, safety risks, and exclusion in workplaces.

Scientific foundation

Research in occupational health and organizational sociology shows that linguistic exclusion undermines coordination, safety, trust, and social belonging at work.

Innovation and design

I conduct randomized field experiments in workplaces and educational settings to test when real-time speech-to-speech translation helps, does nothing, or even hinders coordination.

Why it matters

The project offers causal evidence with immediate relevance for occupational safety, integration, and responsible digitalization in multilingual work environments.

Estimated budget: approximately €40,000

Selected Publications

Snapshot-style summaries of selected papers and books. No direct links, uploads, or reproduced publisher material.

Papers

Paper
Journal of Economic Psychology

Females welcome! When immigrants make the local population happy

Hilke Brockmann and R. Fernandez-Urbano · 2026

This paper examines how immigration shapes the well-being of local populations. It highlights the role of gender composition in migration and shows that female immigration can be associated with more positive happiness effects in receiving societies.

Paper
Journal of Public Policy

Happy taxation: increasing tax compliance through positive rewards?

Hilke Brockmann and collaborators · 2016

This article asks whether positive incentives can improve tax compliance. Rather than focusing only on punishment and deterrence, it explores how reward-based approaches may influence public behavior and perceptions of fairness.

Paper
Journal of Happiness Studies

The China puzzle: falling happiness in a rising economy

Hilke Brockmann and collaborators · 2009

This paper addresses a central sociological question: why can happiness decline even while economic growth accelerates? Using China as a case, it explores the tension between rising prosperity, social change, expectations, and subjective well-being.

Paper
European Sociological Review

Girls preferred? Changing patterns of sex preferences in the two German states

Hilke Brockmann · 2001

This article investigates changing preferences for sons and daughters in East and West Germany. It offers insight into family values, gender norms, and how demographic and cultural change shape reproductive preferences.
