Workshop · IJCAI 2026

2026
Bremen, Germany
Augmented Democracies: How AI Can Empower Political Decision-Making by the People and for the People
Hilke Brockmann & Ivan Yamshchikov · IJCAI 2026

Democratic political regimes are under pressure from declining public trust, polarization, and disinformation, while simultaneously facing external competition from authoritarian systems that increasingly deploy AI for centralized control and persuasion. Recent advances in AI — especially large language models — have created new technical capabilities to support democratic deliberation at scale.

A core challenge in democratic theory is the deliberative trilemma: it is difficult to achieve simultaneously broad participation (scalability), high-quality deliberation, and political equality. Recent AI research suggests that purpose-built AI systems may help relax this trilemma — for example by synthesizing common ground across large and diverse groups, structuring argument exchange, or enabling multilingual participation.

This workshop brings together researchers from AI, HCI, political science, computational social science, and digital democracy practice to critically assess the state of the art and collaboratively design next-generation AI systems that strengthen democratic decision-making by the people and for the people.

Research

I have a multi-disciplinary background in the social sciences. My research focuses on things that matter to people — aging, happiness, inequality, and new data. I usually study large social groups — the elderly, the educated and uneducated, the haves and the have-nots, migrants, and women.

New research methodologies shed new light into the motives and behaviors of people and their (un)intended consequences. I use and combine innovative datasets and employ cutting-edge data science models to study when and why people are happy, what drives the (un)acceptance of economic inequalities, what defines successful migration, and how people can better participate politically.

01
Happiness & Well-Being
Why and when people are happy — across societies, transitions, and inequalities. Combining survey data with new computational methods to understand subjective well-being at scale.
02
Inequality & Redistribution
What drives the acceptance or rejection of economic inequalities. How wealth concentration shapes political attitudes, tax compliance, and social trust.
03
Migration & Integration
What defines successful migration — for newcomers and for host societies. How gender composition of immigrant flows affects subjective well-being in receiving countries.
04
AI & Democracy
How AI and large language models can augment democratic deliberation. Addressing scalability, quality, and equality in political participation — the deliberative trilemma.

Selected Journal Articles
Journal Article Journal of Economic Psychology 2025
Females Welcome! When Immigrants Make the Native Population Happy
Hilke Brockmann & Roger Fernandez-Urbano
Despite growing interest in migration studies, research examining the impact of immigration on the subjective well-being of local populations remains surprisingly limited, often yielding inconclusive and contradictory findings. Notably, the influence of the gender composition of immigrant populations has been overlooked. This study employs the social production function framework to assess how gender-specific immigration patterns affect the well-being of native populations over time, using longitudinal data from Germany and Catalonia (Spain). The study finds that the gender composition of immigrant flows — not just their volume — matters for host-society well-being, with female immigration showing distinct effects from male immigration.
Journal Article Journal of Public Policy Vol. 36 · 2016
Happy Taxation: Increasing Tax Compliance through Positive Rewards?
Hilke Brockmann, Philipp Genschel & Laura Seelkopf
Can governments increase tax compliance by rewarding honest taxpayers? The authors conducted a controlled laboratory experiment comparing tax compliance under a deterrence baseline with two reward treatments: a donation treatment giving taxpayers a say in the spending purposes of their payments, and a lucky treatment giving taxpayers a small chance of winning a lottery. The donation treatment improved compliance only for female participants; the lottery treatment had no significant effect on anyone. The paper concludes that positive rewards can complement traditional deterrence-based tax collection, though a one-size-fits-all approach is unlikely to succeed.
Journal Article Journal of Happiness Studies Vol. 10 · 2009
The China Puzzle: Falling Happiness in a Rising Economy
Hilke Brockmann, Jan Delhey, Christian Welzel & Hao Yuan
Over the 1990–2000 decade happiness in China plummeted despite massive improvement in material living standards — a finding that contradicts the notion that income growth at low living standards leads to gains in happiness. The authors explain this puzzle through the concept of "frustrated achievers": income inequality became increasingly skewed towards the upper strata, so that most Chinese experienced a worsening relative financial position. Consequently, financial dissatisfaction rose and became an increasingly important factor in depressing happiness. The paper concludes with speculations about the applicability of these findings to transition economies in general.
Journal Article European Sociological Review Vol. 17 · 2001
Girls Preferred? Changing Patterns of Sex Preferences in the Two German States
Hilke Brockmann
Taking a longitudinal perspective, this paper shows how sex preferences in Germany changed over time. Based on German cohort data, event-history models reveal a significant boy preference among women born before 1910, a pattern that vanishes among cohorts ten years younger. After the Second World War, West German women never developed a clear sex preference. East German women, by contrast, show a significant preference for girls — a pattern absorbed by the pro-family policy launched by the socialist regime in the 1970s. The paper argues that modernisation does not neutralise sex preferences as often assumed; rather, it may give rise to diverging preferences depending on the specific type of welfare regime.
Books
Book · Editor Edward Elgar Publishing 2024
Encyclopedia of Happiness, Quality of Life and Subjective Wellbeing
Edited by Hilke Brockmann & Roger Fernandez-Urbano · 494 pp. · ISBN 978-1-80088-966-8
This comprehensive Encyclopedia delves into the underpinnings, approaches, and recent advancements in happiness and wellbeing research. Organised into five sections — foundational concepts and disciplinary perspectives; determinants and mechanisms of wellbeing; cultural differences in happiness; trending and emerging topics; and data, methods and measures — the volume brings together contributions from over one hundred scholars, including all six editors of the United Nations World Happiness Report. With a focus on emerging research, it addresses trending topics such as animal happiness, big data, genetic engineering, eco-anxiety, war, and working from home.
Book · Editor Springer · Happiness Studies Book Series 2013
Human Happiness and the Pursuit of Maximization: Is More Always Better?
Edited by Hilke Brockmann & Jan Delhey · ISBN 978-94-007-6608-2
This book tests the critical potential of happiness research to evaluate contemporary high-performance societies — affluent capitalist societies that emphasise competition and success both institutionally and culturally. Using happiness as a reference point, the volume explores the philosophical and empirical limits of the maximisation rule: Why did the idea of economic maximisation gain such ground in Western thinking? When does maximisation work and when does it fail? When do qualities matter more than quantities? Does maximisation yield a different happiness dividend across species, cultures, and societies?

Teaching

My teaching explores how digital technologies reshape society, institutions, behavior, and inequality. I teach across areas including Cybercriminology, Digital Society, and contemporary sociological approaches to technology, crime, and social change.

01

Cybercriminology

Examining cybercrime, online deviance, digital victimization, surveillance, and the social dynamics of crime in technologically mediated environments.

02

Digital Society

Exploring how platforms, data, algorithms, and digital infrastructures shape communication, identity, participation, inequality, and everyday life.

03

Research & Methods

Introducing students to sociological analysis through critical reading, empirical inquiry, and the study of contemporary social issues in digital contexts.

04
